## Reducing Friction

Willpower is limited. Fighting resistance through sheer force works sometimes but not consistently.

A better approach: **make starting so easy that resistance has less to fight against.**

This is about environment design, systems, and lowering the bar so low you almost can't fail to start.

---

## The Role of Environment

Your environment either helps or hinders action.

**Friction creates barriers:**
- Your workspace is cluttered
- The tools you need aren't handy
- Distractions are one click away
- Starting requires decisions

**Reducing friction removes barriers:**
- Everything you need is ready
- Distractions are removed or blocked
- The first step is obvious
- Starting requires no decisions

Same task, same you—but one environment makes action much more likely.

---

## Environment Design Principles

### 1. Prepare in Advance
Set up for tomorrow's work today. Lay out what you'll need. Clear your space. When it's time to work, you just begin.

### 2. Remove Distractions
Phone in another room. Blocking software on. Tab minimized. Notifications off. Make the distracting action harder than the productive action.

### 3. Visual Cues
What you see prompts what you do. Put the project on your desk. Keep the book visible. Out of sight is out of mind.

### 4. Dedicated Space
If possible, have a place for focused work. When you're there, you work. The space primes the behavior.

### 5. Reduce Decisions
Decision fatigue is real. Eliminate choices. Plan what you'll work on. Have defaults. Save your willpower for the work itself.

---

## The Tiny First Step

One of the most powerful anti-procrastination techniques: **make the first step absurdly small.**

**Not:** Write the report.
**Instead:** Open the document.

**Not:** Go to the gym.
**Instead:** Put on workout clothes.

**Not:** Clean the house.
**Instead:** Put one thing away.

The smaller the first step, the less resistance can fight it. And once you've started, momentum often carries you further.

## Systems Over Willpower

**Willpower** says: "Just try harder."
**Systems** say: "Change the conditions so you don't need to try so hard."

Systems that help:
- **Time blocking:** Specific times for specific work. When the time comes, you don't decide—you do.
- **Rituals:** The same sequence before work. Coffee, music, desk setup. The ritual triggers the mode.
- **Accountability:** Someone checking in. External structure when internal structure fails.
- **Deadlines:** Real ones. Artificial ones if needed. Pressure creates action.
- **Batching:** Similar tasks together. Once you're in the mode, keep doing.

---

## Implementation Intentions

Research shows that deciding **when and where** you'll do something dramatically increases follow-through.

**Vague:** "I'll work on the project this week."
**Specific:** "I'll work on the project Tuesday at 9am at my desk."

When the time and place arrive, the decision is already made. You just execute.

Formula: "I will [BEHAVIOR] at [TIME] in [LOCATION]."

## The First 15 Minutes

The hardest part is the first 15 minutes. After that, you're usually in flow.

**Strategies for the first 15:**
- No distractions. None. Block everything.
- Start with something engaging (if possible)
- Just survive those minutes. The rest is easier.
- Use a timer. Watch it count. Just make it through.

If you can make it through the first 15 minutes, you've probably won.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Environment shapes behavior.** Design for action, not against it.

- **Reduce friction.** Make starting require less effort and fewer decisions.

- **Tiny first steps** bypass resistance.

- **Systems beat willpower.** Change conditions instead of relying on force.
