## The Skill of Feeling

Most people skip this step. They either suppress anger before it's fully felt, or they jump straight to action (usually destructive).

But between stimulus and response, there's a crucial space: the space of actually feeling.

**Feeling anger fully—in your body, without judgment—is the foundation of healthy anger.**

---

## Why Feeling Matters

When you actually feel anger:

- The emotion can complete its cycle
- The nervous system can regulate
- You get the information the emotion carries
- You can choose your response consciously
- You don't need to stuff or explode

When you don't feel anger:

- It gets stuck in your body
- It builds up pressure
- It comes out sideways or explosively
- You miss important information
- Chronic tension and resentment develop

---

## Anger in the Body

Anger is a physical experience. Before it's a thought or action, it's sensation.

Common places anger shows up:

- **Jaw:** clenching, tension
- **Neck and shoulders:** tightening, raising
- **Chest:** heat, expansion, pressure
- **Arms and hands:** tension, desire to push or hit
- **Belly:** churning, tightening
- **Overall:** heat rising, energy mobilizing

Your anger has a unique body signature. Learning to recognize it is the first step to feeling it fully.

## The Difference: Feeling vs. Fueling

There's a crucial distinction between:

**Feeling anger:** Allowing sensations to be present, observing them, breathing with them.

**Fueling anger:** Feeding the thoughts, building a case, rehearsing grievances, making it bigger.

Feeling completes the cycle. Fueling keeps you stuck.

When you feel anger, you might:
- Notice the heat and let it be
- Observe thoughts without attaching
- Stay present with physical sensations
- Breathe and allow

When you fuel anger, you might:
- Replay the situation over and over
- Build your case for why you're right
- Imagine confrontations or revenge
- Tell the story to everyone who will listen
- Keep adding evidence to the pile

**The goal is feeling without fueling.**

---

## A Practice for Feeling Anger

Try this when anger arises:

**1. Pause**
Stop before acting. Create space.

**2. Acknowledge**
"I'm feeling anger right now."

**3. Locate**
Where is it in my body? What sensations?

**4. Allow**
Let the sensations be there. Don't push them away or make them bigger.

**5. Breathe**
Slow, full breaths. Breath can be into the area where you feel the anger.

**6. Observe**
Notice how sensations change, shift, move. They're not static.

**7. Wait**
Give it time. Emotions are waves—they rise, peak, and fall.

**8. Listen**
Once the intensity decreases, notice: What is this anger telling me?

## Common Obstacles to Feeling

Several things can get in the way of actually feeling anger:

### Fear
"If I really feel this, I'll lose control / hurt someone / become a bad person."

**Truth:** Feeling is not acting. You can feel intense anger without doing anything destructive. Suppressing is more likely to lead to loss of control later.

### Shame
"I shouldn't be angry. Good people don't feel this."

**Truth:** Anger is a normal human emotion. Feeling it doesn't make you bad. You have a right to feel anything.

### Discomfort
"This is uncomfortable. I want it to stop."

**Truth:** Yes, it's uncomfortable. But discomfort is not the same as danger. You can tolerate uncomfortable sensations—they pass.

### Urgency
"I have to DO something right now!"

**Truth:** You rarely have to act immediately. The urgency is the emotion talking, not reality. You can feel first, act later.

---

## When Not to Dive Deep

Sometimes it's not appropriate to fully feel anger in the moment:

- In a professional setting
- When you need to stay functional
- When you're already dysregulated
- When the situation is unsafe

In these cases:
1. Acknowledge the anger ("I'm feeling anger")
2. Make a mental note to process it later
3. Use regulation tools to stay functional
4. Return to fully feel it when it's safe

The key is not suppressing permanently—just delaying until the right time.

---

## Titration: Working with Big Anger

If your anger is very intense or connected to trauma, feeling it all at once might be overwhelming.

**Titration** means working with small amounts at a time:

- Touch into the anger briefly (10-30 seconds)
- Notice sensations
- Pull back and regulate (breathing, grounding)
- Return when you're ready
- Gradually increase how long you stay with it

This is especially important if:
- Your anger connects to trauma
- You've suppressed for a long time
- The intensity feels overwhelming
- You're not sure you can stay present

Consider working with a therapist if your anger feels too big to work with alone.

## This Week's Practice

1. **Notice anger when it arises** this week—even small irritations.

2. **Pause and feel** before reacting. Even 30 seconds of feeling makes a difference.

3. **Track the body sensations.** Get to know your anger signature.

4. **Don't judge yourself.** This is practice. It takes time.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Feeling vs. Fueling:** Allowing sensations to be present without feeding the mental story.

- **Anger Wave:** Emotions rise, peak, and fall. If you stay with the feeling, it will shift on its own.

- **Titration:** Working with intense emotions in small doses, with regulation in between.
