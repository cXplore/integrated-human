## Why Do We React?

Sometimes emotions seem to come out of nowhere—suddenly you're angry, anxious, or hurt. But emotions don't appear randomly. They're triggered by something—external events, internal thoughts, or situations that activate learned patterns. Understanding your triggers gives you power.

---

## What Are Triggers?

### Definition

Emotional triggers are:
- Stimuli that activate emotional responses
- Often rapid and automatic
- Linked to past experiences
- Not always obvious
- Unique to each person

### The Trigger Process

How it works:
1. Something happens (trigger)
2. Your brain interprets it (usually unconsciously)
3. Emotion activates
4. Body responds
5. Behavior follows

### Often Below Awareness

Many triggers operate:
- Without conscious awareness
- Faster than thinking
- Based on pattern matching
- From old learning

---

## Types of Triggers

### External Triggers

Things in your environment:
- What someone says
- Tone of voice
- Specific situations
- Places or settings
- Certain people
- Sounds, smells, sights

### Internal Triggers

Things inside you:
- Thoughts and memories
- Physical sensations
- Other emotions
- Fatigue or hunger
- Stress levels

### Relational Triggers

In relationships:
- Feeling ignored
- Being criticized
- Perceived rejection
- Conflict dynamics
- Intimacy or distance

## The Stories Behind Triggers

### Why These Triggers?

Your triggers developed because:
- Past experiences created associations
- Similar situations were painful before
- You learned certain things mean danger
- Old wounds get re-activated
- The past lives in the present

### The Interpretation Layer

Between trigger and emotion:
- An interpretation happens
- Usually automatic and instant
- "This means..."
- Based on past learning
- May not be accurate now

### Example

The trigger: Someone doesn't respond to your text

Possible interpretations:
- "They're ignoring me" → hurt, anger
- "They're probably busy" → neutral
- "Did I say something wrong?" → anxiety
- "They don't care about me" → sadness

Same trigger, different interpretations, different emotions.

## Common Trigger Themes

### Core Sensitivities

Many triggers relate to core concerns:
- **Rejection:** Being excluded, unwanted
- **Failure:** Not being good enough
- **Abandonment:** Being left alone
- **Loss of control:** Helplessness
- **Unfairness:** Being treated unjustly
- **Disrespect:** Not being valued
- **Shame:** Being exposed, humiliated

### Your Themes

Most people have 1-3 core themes that many triggers connect to. What are yours?

## Working with Triggers

### Awareness First

The first step is simply:
- Recognizing you're triggered
- Noticing the pattern
- Catching it as it happens
- Creating a moment of awareness

### The Pause

When triggered:
- Pause before reacting
- Take a breath
- Notice what you're feeling
- Name the trigger
- Choose your response

### Questioning the Interpretation

Ask yourself:
- Is this interpretation accurate?
- What else could this mean?
- Am I reacting to now or to the past?
- What response would serve me?

---

## Creating a Trigger Map

### Your Personal Map

A trigger map includes:
- Your common triggers
- The emotions they produce
- The interpretations behind them
- The themes they connect to
- Alternative interpretations

### Using Your Map

With a map, you can:
- Anticipate triggers
- Prepare responses
- Catch yourself faster
- Choose different interpretations
- Reduce reactivity over time

## Key Concepts

- **Triggers:** Stimuli that activate emotional responses, often automatic
- **Types:** External (environment), internal (thoughts/sensations), relational
- **Interpretation layer:** The story between trigger and emotion
- **Core themes:** Underlying sensitivities like rejection, failure, shame
- **The pause:** Creating space between trigger and response
- **Questioning:** Challenging automatic interpretations
- **Trigger map:** Tool for anticipating and managing triggers

---

<Callout type="important">
Triggers aren't the enemy—they're information. They reveal what matters to you, where you've been hurt, and what interpretations you automatically make. Understanding your triggers doesn't mean you won't be triggered; it means you can catch yourself faster and choose your response more consciously.
</Callout>

*Know your triggers, and they lose some of their power.*
