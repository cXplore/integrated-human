## The Absence of Presence

Most people aren't fully present during sex.

They're in their heads—worrying about performance, wondering what their partner thinks, planning what to do next, or somewhere else entirely.

**The body is there, but the person is gone.**

---

## Performance vs. Presence

### Performance Orientation:
- Focus on doing it "right"
- Worried about how you look
- Goal-oriented (toward orgasm or "success")
- In your head, not your body
- Trying to be good rather than feel good
- Sexual experience as accomplishment

### Presence Orientation:
- Focus on sensation and connection
- Fully in the experience
- Process-oriented (enjoying what's happening)
- In your body, feeling
- Being rather than doing
- Sexual experience as exploration

---

## The Cost of Performance

### What Performance Does:
- Creates anxiety (am I doing it right?)
- Disconnects from sensation (can't feel when thinking)
- Blocks intimacy (partner connects with performance, not you)
- Reduces pleasure (can't enjoy what you're not present for)
- Creates pressure (especially around orgasm)

### The Cycle:
Performance anxiety → Less sensation → Less pleasure → More anxiety → Worse experience

## What Presence Looks Like

### During Sexual Experience:
- **In the body:** Feeling sensations as they happen
- **Here and now:** Not in past or future
- **With partner:** Sensing their experience, meeting them
- **Allowing:** Not forcing or controlling
- **Responsive:** Following what feels good
- **Connected:** To yourself, to partner, to the moment

### The Shift:
From "How am I doing?" to "What am I feeling?"

---

## Why Presence Is Difficult

### Common Obstacles:

**Spectatoring:**
Watching yourself from outside, judging

**Future focus:**
Thinking about orgasm, what comes next

**Past thinking:**
Comparing to previous experiences

**Body judgment:**
Worried about how you look

**Performance pressure:**
Trying to "do it right"

**Dissociation:**
Checking out entirely (often from trauma)

---

## Building Presence

### The Fundamentals:

**1. Attention on Sensation**
Instead of thinking, feel. What sensations are happening in your body right now?

**2. Breath**
Full breathing keeps you in the body. Holding breath takes you out.

**3. Slow Down**
Speed pulls you into performance. Slowing allows presence.

**4. Let Go of Goals**
Release orgasm as the goal. Be in the experience.

**5. Stay Curious**
"What does this feel like?" replaces "Am I doing this right?"

## Sensate Focus

### A Practice for Building Presence:

Developed by Masters and Johnson, sensate focus is structured touching without goal:

**The Basic Practice:**
1. Take turns touching/being touched
2. Focus on sensation only
3. No genital contact initially
4. No goal (no orgasm required)
5. Simply feel and be felt

**Why It Works:**
- Removes performance pressure
- Trains attention on sensation
- Builds body awareness
- Creates intimacy

### Can Practice Solo:
Touch yourself with pure attention to sensation—not to arouse, just to feel.

---

## Breath and Sexuality

### The Connection:
Breath is directly connected to sexual experience:
- Shallow breathing = less sensation
- Held breath = anxiety, moving toward goal
- Full breathing = more aliveness, more sensation
- Connected breath = builds and moves energy

### The Practice:
During sexual experience, notice breath:
- Are you breathing?
- Is it shallow?
- Can you deepen it?
- Breathe into sensation

---

## Slowing Down

### Speed and Presence:
Fast sex is usually performance sex. You can't be present at high speed.

### The Practice:
- Deliberately slow down
- Pause during sexual experience
- Linger in moments
- Don't rush toward climax

### What Happens:
Slowing creates space for presence, sensation, and connection. More is felt. Pleasure deepens.

## Presence with a Partner

### Creating Presence Together:

**Eye Contact:**
Looking into each other's eyes creates profound presence (and vulnerability).

**Verbal Checking In:**
"What are you feeling?" "What do you want?"

**Synchronized Breath:**
Breathing together creates connection.

**Pausing Together:**
Stopping in the midst of experience to feel, connect, be together.

**Naming Experience:**
"This feels..." Putting words to experience.

---

## When Presence Is Difficult

### If You Dissociate:
Leaving the body during sex is common, especially with trauma history.

**What Helps:**
- Grounding practices (feel feet, name objects)
- Eyes open
- Communication ("I'm checking out")
- Slower, more controlled pace
- Trauma therapy to address root cause

### If Anxiety Takes Over:
Anxiety is presence's enemy.

**What Helps:**
- Return to breath
- Ground in sensation
- Communicate to partner
- Address underlying anxiety sources

---

## Solo Practice

### Presence Begins Alone:
Building presence in solo sexuality:
- Self-touch with attention
- Not goal-focused
- Exploring sensation
- Breathing fully
- No performance for anyone

### Why It Matters:
If you can't be present with yourself, it's hard to be present with another.

## The Reward of Presence

### What Presence Brings:
- More pleasure (actually feel what's happening)
- Deeper connection (truly with partner)
- Less anxiety (not performing)
- More satisfaction (not chasing goal)
- Greater intimacy (meeting authentically)
- Sexual experiences as meditation, not performance

---

## Key Concepts

- **Performance vs. Presence:** Doing vs. being; thinking vs. feeling.

- **Performance Costs:** Anxiety, disconnection, reduced pleasure.

- **Building Presence:** Sensation focus, breath, slowing down, releasing goals.

- **Obstacles:** Spectatoring, goal-focus, anxiety, dissociation.

- **The Reward:** More pleasure, deeper connection, greater satisfaction.
