## Building Better Sleep

You've learned why sleep matters, how it works, and what disrupts it.

Now: how do you actually optimize it? This module provides evidence-based protocols for improving sleep quality and duration.

**Small changes, consistently applied, create significant improvements.**

---

## The Sleep Environment

### The Bedroom Should Be:

**Dark:**
- Blackout curtains or blackout blinds
- No light from electronics
- Cover any standby lights
- Eye mask if needed

**Cool:**
- 65-68°F (18-20°C)
- Adjust bedding seasonally
- Consider fans or AC
- Breathable materials

**Quiet:**
- Minimize noise
- White noise machine or app if needed
- Earplugs if necessary
- Address snoring partner issues

**Comfortable:**
- Quality mattress (replace every 7-10 years)
- Comfortable pillows
- Fresh, breathable bedding
- Minimize clutter

## Light Management

### Morning Light:
- Get bright light exposure within 30 minutes of waking
- Natural sunlight is best
- Light box if needed (especially winter)
- This sets your circadian rhythm

### Evening Light:
- Dim lights 1-2 hours before bed
- Avoid overhead lights; use lamps
- Blue light filters on all devices
- Consider "night mode" or f.lux software

### Why This Works:
Morning light = "wake up" signal to brain
Evening darkness = melatonin release begins

---

## Consistent Schedule

### The Most Important Factor:
Consistent sleep/wake times regulate your circadian rhythm.

### The Protocol:
- Same wake time every day (within 30-60 minutes)
- Same bedtime every night (within 30-60 minutes)
- Yes, weekends too
- Anchor wake time is most important

### Social Jet Lag:
If you sleep two hours later on weekends, it's like flying two time zones and back every week.

---

## Wind-Down Routine

### Why You Need One:
Your brain needs transition from waking activity to sleep. A consistent routine signals it's time to wind down.

### Components of a Wind-Down:
- Begin 30-60 minutes before bed
- Same activities in same order each night
- Calm, relaxing activities
- No screens, no work, no stressful conversations

### Example Routine:
1. Dim lights
2. Herbal tea or warm drink
3. Light reading (physical book)
4. Gentle stretching or yoga
5. Personal hygiene routine
6. Bed

## Sleep Hygiene Protocols

### Pre-Sleep:
- No caffeine after noon
- No alcohol within 3 hours of bed
- No large meals within 2-3 hours
- No intense exercise within 2-3 hours
- No screens within 1 hour (ideal)

### The Bed Is For:
- Sleep
- Sex
- Nothing else

### If you work, watch TV, or scroll in bed, your brain associates bed with wakefulness.

### If You Can't Sleep:
- Don't lie in bed awake for more than 20 minutes
- Get up, do something calm in dim light
- Return to bed when sleepy
- Don't watch the clock

---

## Timing Your Sleep

### Finding Your Window:
When does your body naturally want to sleep?

### Signs of Your Natural Window:
- When you feel genuinely sleepy (not just tired)
- When you'd sleep if left to your own schedule
- Your chronotype (lark vs. owl)

### Working with Your Type:
If possible, align schedule with your natural rhythms. If not possible, consistent schedule helps shift rhythm.

## Supplements and Aids

### Melatonin:
- Can help with timing issues (jet lag, shift work)
- Not a sedative—signals sleep time
- Start low (0.5-1mg)
- Not for long-term use typically
- Doesn't fix underlying issues

### Magnesium:
- Supports relaxation
- Many people are deficient
- Magnesium glycinate best for sleep
- Safe for most people

### Other Supplements:
- L-theanine: calming
- Glycine: may improve sleep quality
- Valerian, chamomile: mild effects

### Warning:
- Sleep aids (prescription or OTC) don't provide natural sleep architecture
- Can be habit-forming
- Address root causes rather than masking with medications

## The Sleep Restriction Technique

### For Chronic Insomnia:
If you're in bed 8 hours but only sleeping 5, sleep efficiency is low.

### The Protocol:
1. Calculate your actual sleep time
2. Restrict time in bed to that amount
3. Maintain consistent wake time
4. As sleep efficiency improves, gradually increase time in bed
5. Builds sleep pressure and reconsolidates sleep

### Note:
This is a simplified version of CBT-I (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia). For chronic insomnia, work with a professional.

## Tracking Sleep

### Options:
- Sleep diary (simple, free)
- Wearable trackers (Whoop, Oura, Apple Watch)
- Smartphone apps

### What to Track:
- Time to bed, time to wake
- How long to fall asleep
- Night awakenings
- How you feel in morning
- Factors that might affect (caffeine, stress, etc.)

### Purpose:
Identifying patterns and measuring improvement.

### Caveat:
Don't become obsessive about tracking. Anxiety about sleep data can worsen sleep.

---

## Making Changes Stick

### Start Small:
Don't change everything at once. Pick 1-2 changes.

### Be Consistent:
One week isn't enough. Give changes 2-4 weeks.

### Be Patient:
Sleep improvement is often gradual.

### Troubleshoot:
If something isn't working, adjust and try again.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Environment:** Dark, cool, quiet, comfortable.

- **Light Management:** Morning light, evening darkness.

- **Consistency:** Same times every day—anchor wake time.

- **Wind-Down:** Routine that signals sleep time.

- **Sleep Hygiene:** Protocols that support natural sleep.
