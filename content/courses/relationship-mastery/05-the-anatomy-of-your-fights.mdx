## Your Fights Have a Structure

You know how your fights go.

There's a pattern. A sequence. A predictable arc that you've traveled many times, even if the topic changes.

Someone says something. Someone reacts. It escalates. Someone withdraws or attacks. It ends the same way it always ends.

You can probably predict, three moves in, exactly where this fight is heading.

**This predictability is your opportunity.** Once you can see the structure, you can interrupt it.

---

## The Fight You Keep Having

Most couples have the same fight over and over. The content changes — it might be about dishes one day and money the next — but the underlying structure is identical.

This is because you're not really fighting about dishes or money. You're fighting about needs, fears, and patterns that run beneath the surface content.

## The Four Horsemen (Revisited)

You've heard of these. Now we look at how they show up in YOUR fights.

### Criticism
Attacking character rather than addressing behavior.
"You never..." "You always..." "You're so..."

### Contempt
Disgust, mockery, eye-rolling, superiority.
The single most destructive pattern in relationships.

### Defensiveness
Protecting rather than listening. Counter-attacking. "But you..."

### Stonewalling
Withdrawing. Shutting down. Leaving (emotionally or physically).

## The Negative Cycle

Every couple develops a negative cycle — a self-reinforcing pattern of attack and defense.

**Example:**
- Partner A criticizes
- Partner B defends
- Partner A escalates
- Partner B stonewalls
- Partner A pursues/attacks more
- Partner B shuts down completely
- Partner A feels abandoned
- Partner B feels suffocated

The cycle perpetuates itself. Each response triggers the other's response.

**Neither person is the villain.** Both are caught in the cycle, doing what feels necessary given what the other is doing.

## The Fight Beneath the Fight

The content of your fights is rarely the real issue.

**Surface fight:** "You never help with dishes"
**Underneath:** "I feel alone in this partnership. I need to know I matter to you."

**Surface fight:** "You're always on my case"
**Underneath:** "I feel like I'm never good enough for you. I need acceptance."

## Interrupt Points

Once you see the structure, you can identify where to interrupt it.

**Every fight has moments where it could go differently.** Points where, if you did something different, the whole sequence would change.

### Common Interrupt Points:

**At the trigger:**
Before anything is said. You notice you're activated. You could regulate instead of react.

**At the first response:**
They said something triggering. You could pause instead of counter-attacking.

**At the escalation:**
It's getting heated. You could call for a break instead of matching their intensity.

**At the horseman:**
You're about to criticize, show contempt, defend, or stonewall. You could choose a different response.

**At the spiral:**
You're in the negative cycle. You could name it instead of continuing it.

## The Alternative Moves

For each horseman, there's an antidote. For each destructive move, there's an alternative.

### Instead of Criticism → Soft Start-Up
"When [specific situation], I felt [feeling]. I need [need]. Would you be willing to [request]?"

### Instead of Contempt → Appreciation
Remember what you love. Speak it. Even in conflict, maintain fundamental respect.

### Instead of Defensiveness → Taking Responsibility
"You're right that I..." or "I can see how that landed for you..."
Take your 2% (or 50%), even if you don't agree with everything.

### Instead of Stonewalling → Regulated Break
"I'm flooding. I need 30 minutes. I'll be back and we'll continue."

## The Meta-Move: Naming the Cycle

One of the most powerful things you can do mid-fight:

**Name what's happening.**

"I notice we're doing our thing. I'm getting critical and you're withdrawing. Can we stop for a second?"

"Wait. I'm flooding. I'm about to say something I don't mean. Can we take a break?"

"I think we're not actually fighting about dishes. This is about something bigger. Can we talk about what's really going on?"

This moves you from INSIDE the cycle to OUTSIDE it. From fighting TO observing the fight together.

## Perpetual Problems

Remember: 69% of relationship conflicts are perpetual. They never get fully resolved.

These are differences in:
- Values
- Personality
- Lifestyle preferences
- Life goals

**The goal isn't to solve perpetual problems.** It's to have ongoing dialogue about them without gridlock.

Signs of gridlock:
- You've stopped talking about it
- The issue feels stuck
- You feel hurt and hopeless about it
- You've begun vilifying your partner over it

Moving out of gridlock:
- Understand the dreams/values underneath each position
- Accept that you won't fully agree
- Find temporary, workable compromises
- Keep the dialogue open
