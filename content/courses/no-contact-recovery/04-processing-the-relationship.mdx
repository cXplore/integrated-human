## Two Distortions to Overcome

After a breakup, your brain distorts the past in two ways:

**Idealization:** Remembering only the good. The love, the connection, the best moments. Forgetting the fights, the pain, the incompatibilities.

**Demonization:** Remembering only the bad. They become a villain. Everything was terrible. You were a victim the whole time.

Neither is accurate. Reality is more complex—and more useful.

This module is about seeing clearly.

---

## Why Your Memory Lies

Memory isn't a recording—it's a reconstruction. And your current state affects what you "remember."

**When you miss them:**
Your brain serves up highlight reels. The good times feel more real than the bad. You forget why you were unhappy.

**When you're angry:**
Your brain emphasizes every wound. The good times seem like manipulation. You forget there was real love.

**What's actually true:**
The relationship contained both. Real connection AND real problems. Genuine love AND genuine dysfunction. You can hold both.

---

## The Complete Picture

To heal, you need to see the full relationship—not the fantasy version or the nightmare version.

**The good that was real:**
- What did you genuinely love about them?
- What moments of real connection did you share?
- What did they bring to your life that was positive?
- What did you learn from them?

**The problems that were also real:**
- What consistently didn't work?
- Where were you fundamentally incompatible?
- What patterns kept repeating?
- What did you sacrifice to make it work?

**Your part in it:**
- What did you bring that wasn't healthy?
- Where did you abandon yourself?
- What red flags did you ignore?
- What patterns from your history did you repeat?

This isn't about blame—it's about honesty.

---

## Common Relationship Autopsy Mistakes

### Mistake 1: "It was perfect until..."
No, it wasn't. You just hadn't hit the issues yet. Look earlier—the seeds were there.

### Mistake 2: "If only I had..."
You couldn't have fixed fundamental incompatibility by being better. Stop torturing yourself with counterfactuals.

### Mistake 3: "They're entirely to blame."
You chose to stay. You had your own patterns. Owning your part isn't letting them off the hook—it's taking your power back.

### Mistake 4: "I'm entirely to blame."
You didn't cause their behavior. Two people create a dynamic. You're responsible for your half, not their half.

### Mistake 5: "I'll never know why."
You probably already know. You're just not ready to accept it yet.

---

## The Relationship Inventory

Time to get specific.

**Non-negotiables that were violated:**
What did you need from a partner that you consistently didn't get? What boundaries were crossed repeatedly?

**Compatibility issues:**
Where were you fundamentally different in ways that couldn't be bridged? Values, life goals, communication styles, needs.

**Red flags you ignored:**
What did you see early on that you explained away? What did your gut tell you that you didn't listen to?

**Patterns that repeated:**
What dynamics kept showing up? The same fights? The same withdrawals? The same disappointments?

**What you abandoned for the relationship:**
Friends? Hobbies? Self-respect? Your own needs? What did you give up to make it work?

## Releasing the Fantasy

One of the hardest things to grieve is the future you imagined.

**What you're also mourning:**
- The wedding that won't happen
- The home you won't share
- The kids you won't have together
- The adventures you planned
- The person you thought they were
- The person you thought you'd become with them

**The truth:** You're not just grieving them as they are—you're grieving a fantasy. An imagined future that was never guaranteed.

This is important to recognize. Because you're not losing something you had—you're losing something you imagined.

---

## The Story You Tell Matters

How you narrativize this relationship will shape your future.

**Victim story:** "They ruined me. I'll never trust again. People always leave."
This keeps you stuck. It gives away your power.

**Growth story:** "That was painful, and I learned a lot. I now know myself better. I know what I need. I won't repeat those patterns."
This moves you forward. It reclaims your power.

The story isn't about denying pain. It's about not making the pain the whole story.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Memory lies.** Both idealization and demonization distort the truth.

- **Reality is complex.** The relationship had real good AND real problems.

- **Own your part.** This is power, not self-blame.

- **The lessons are gold.** Extract meaning from the pain.
