## The Family Dance

Family dynamics are like a dance. Everyone has their steps. The steps are choreographed through years of practice. Each person's moves prompt the next person's moves, which prompt the next, in an endless loop.

Mom criticizes. Dad withdraws. Child acts out. Mom focuses on child. Dad feels relieved. Mom criticizes. And round it goes.

Here's the insight: You can't change anyone else's steps. But you can change yours. And when you change your steps, the dance has to change.

---

## Why You Can Only Change Your Part

### You Don't Control Others

This is obvious but easily forgotten. You cannot make another person:
- See things differently
- Feel differently
- Behave differently
- Heal their wounds
- Take responsibility

You can hope, wish, ask, demand—but ultimately, their change is their choice.

Trying to change others is exhausting and usually fails. It often makes things worse.

### The System Follows

But here's what you can do: change yourself.

And when you change, the system must respond. If you stop playing your part, the dance can't continue the same way. Others must adapt.

They might not adapt the way you want. They might resist, escalate, or disconnect. But they cannot continue unchanged when you've changed.

### Your Power Is Here

Your power isn't in changing others. It's in changing you.

This feels less satisfying. We want others to change. We're sure they're the problem.

But your part is where your power lives.

---

## Identifying Your Part

### The Patterns You're In

What are the repetitive patterns in your family? The arguments that happen again and again. The dynamics that replay. The predictable sequences.

In each pattern, what's your part?

**Common parts:**
- The pursuer (seeking connection that the other avoids)
- The distancer (withdrawing when pursued)
- The overfunctioner (doing more than your share)
- The underfunctioner (doing less than your share)
- The conflict-seeker (escalating tensions)
- The conflict-avoider (smoothing over problems)
- The triangled party (in the middle of others' issues)
- The fixer (trying to solve everyone's problems)

You probably play the same parts across different relationships.

### What You Get From Your Part

Your part serves you somehow, or you wouldn't keep playing it.

The pursuer feels connected (even if rejected).
The distancer feels safe (even if lonely).
The overfunctioner feels in control (even if exhausted).
The fixer feels needed (even if unappreciated).

What do you get from your part? What would you have to face if you stopped playing it?

### What Your Part Costs

Your part also costs you.

The pursuer never gets satisfying connection.
The distancer never gets genuine intimacy.
The overfunctioner burns out.
The fixer enables others' dysfunction.

What does your part cost you? What would you gain if you stopped?

## How to Change Your Part

### 1. Observe Without Acting

First, just observe. Notice when the pattern starts. Notice your impulse to play your part. Notice what happens in your body.

Don't try to change yet. Just observe.

"There it is. Mom said that thing. I feel the pull to defend myself. My chest is tight. I notice the old words forming."

Observation creates a gap between stimulus and response.

### 2. Identify the Choice Point

In every pattern, there's a moment of choice—even if it doesn't feel like one.

Someone does something. You have a reaction. There's a moment before you act on the reaction.

That moment is your choice point. In that moment, you can do your usual part—or something different.

The goal is to lengthen that moment. To find it. To inhabit it.

### 3. Choose a Different Response

What would be different from your usual part?

If you usually pursue → Try giving space
If you usually distance → Try staying present
If you usually overfunction → Try doing less
If you usually fix → Try letting them struggle
If you usually defend → Try not defending
If you usually attack → Try expressing vulnerability

The different response should feel uncomfortable. If it feels easy, it's probably not different enough.

### 4. Hold the Discomfort

When you don't play your part, discomfort arises. The pattern wants to complete. Others will try to pull you back into role.

Hold the discomfort. Don't explain, justify, or process out loud. Just do something different and tolerate the tension.

This is the hardest part. Your nervous system will scream to go back to familiar. Stay with the different.

### 5. Expect Pushback

The system doesn't want you to change. Expect:

- Confusion ("What's wrong with you?")
- Pressure ("Why are you being weird?")
- Escalation (they do their part bigger)
- Criticism ("You're being selfish")
- Guilt-tripping ("After everything I've done...")

This pushback is proof you're changing something. If there were no pushback, you probably haven't changed enough.

### 6. Stay Connected

Here's the key: change your part while maintaining connection.

If you change by withdrawing, cutting off, or being hostile—that's not differentiated change. That's another form of reactivity.

The goal: stay calm, stay present, stay connected—while not playing your old part.

"I love you AND I'm not going to do that."

## What to Expect

When you change your part:

**Initially:**
- Intense discomfort
- Others' confusion or pushback
- Strong pull back to old patterns
- Feeling like you're doing something wrong
- Possible escalation of the old dynamic

**Over time (if you persist):**
- The old pattern loses power
- Others may begin to shift (or not)
- You feel more free
- The dynamic becomes less charged
- New patterns may emerge

**Long term:**
- Old triggers lose their charge
- You respond rather than react
- Relationships may improve or naturally fall away
- You're no longer trapped in the dance

## Common Mistakes

### Changing to Change Others

If you change your part primarily to get others to change, you're still trying to control them. That's not differentiation—it's manipulation.

Change because it's the right change for you, regardless of how others respond.

### Announcing Your Change

"I'm not going to play this role anymore!" This usually creates drama rather than change.

Just quietly do something different. Let actions speak.

### Explaining Your Change

You don't owe explanations for not playing your old part. In fact, explaining often pulls you back into the pattern.

You can simply do something different without justifying it.

### Expecting Quick Results

Family patterns took decades to form. They don't shift in one conversation.

This is ongoing work. You'll need to not play your part many times before the pattern really shifts.

## Key Concepts

- **The family dance:** Patterns are choreographed with each person's steps
- **You can only change your part:** You can't control others, but you can change yourself
- **Choice point:** The moment between trigger and response
- **Holding discomfort:** Tolerating the tension of not playing your part
- **Expect pushback:** The system resists change
- **Stay connected:** Change while maintaining relationship
