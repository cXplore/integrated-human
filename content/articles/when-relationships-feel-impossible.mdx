Maybe you've been hurt too many times. Maybe every relationship has ended the same way. Maybe you're exhausted from trying, from hoping, from getting it wrong.

Or maybe you look at other people's connections and feel like you're watching through glass—like there's something they know how to do that you were never taught.

If relationships feel impossible right now, you're not wrong. For you, right now, with your history and your wounds and your exhausted nervous system—connection probably does feel impossible. That doesn't mean you're broken. It means you're being honest about where you are.

## What's Actually Happening

Attachment patterns form early. The way you learned to connect (or protect yourself from connection) was shaped before you had words for it.

When those patterns were painful—when love came with conditions, or came and went unpredictably, or never came at all—your nervous system learned that closeness equals danger.

Now, even when you want connection, your body might:
- Shut down when someone gets too close
- Push people away before they can leave
- Cling so hard you squeeze the life out of relationships
- Choose people who confirm your worst beliefs about love
- Feel nothing where feelings should be

This isn't a character flaw. It's survival programming. And it made sense once, even if it's hurting you now.

## What This Loneliness Is

The loneliness of attachment wounds is particular. It's not just "I wish I had more friends." It's:
- Feeling alone even in a room full of people
- Wanting connection while being terrified of it
- Believing you're fundamentally unlovable
- Exhaustion from performing closeness you don't feel
- Shame about your history with relationships

This kind of loneliness can make you want to give up entirely. To decide you're better off alone, that you don't need anyone, that independence is strength.

And maybe that's true for now. Maybe pulling back is what you need to do while you heal. But I want to offer another possibility.

## What If It's Not You?

What if the reason relationships have been hard isn't because you're broken—but because you never had the chance to learn?

Connection is a skill. It's taught (or not) in childhood, through hundreds of small moments of attunement and rupture and repair. If those moments didn't happen, or happened wrong, you didn't get the training.

You wouldn't blame yourself for not speaking a language you were never taught. Why blame yourself for not knowing how to connect when no one showed you how?

## The Smallest Step

You don't have to fix your attachment patterns today. You don't have to have a relationship or even want one.

But if some part of you is still hoping, here's the smallest step:

**Notice one moment of connection that doesn't scare you.**

Maybe it's:
- A cashier who makes eye contact and smiles
- A pet who's happy to see you
- A character in a book or show who you understand
- An online community where you feel less alone
- A moment in nature where you feel held

These count. These tiny connections are practice. They're your nervous system learning, slowly, that contact doesn't always mean pain.

## What Healing Looks Like

Healing attachment wounds doesn't mean becoming good at relationships overnight. It means:
- Understanding why you do what you do
- Having compassion for the child who learned to protect themselves
- Slowly, carefully, experimenting with trust
- Finding people who can hold your story without flinching
- Reparenting yourself in the ways you weren't parented

This takes time. Often years. And it's not linear—you'll have moments of openness followed by retreat, connection followed by fear.

That's okay. That's what healing looks like.

## A Truth About Being Alone

Some people do choose solitude, genuinely and freely, and thrive. If that's you—if you've done the work and isolation truly serves you—this article isn't arguing against that.

But if you're choosing isolation because connection hurts too much, that's different. That's not freedom, it's a cage. A comfortable one, maybe, but still a cage.

The question isn't "Should I want relationships?" The question is: "Am I choosing this, or is my fear choosing for me?"

## What You Might Need

If relationships feel impossible, you might need:
- A therapist who understands attachment (this is learnable in relationship)
- Time to grieve what you didn't get
- Permission to go slow
- People who prove your beliefs wrong, one small moment at a time
- Self-compassion when you screw up

You might not be ready for any of this yet. That's okay too. Sometimes we need to sit in the impossible for a while before we can imagine anything different.
